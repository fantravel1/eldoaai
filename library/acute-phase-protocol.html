Acute Phase Protocol

Quick Reference (Pocketbook)

Easy Understanding: When you first get hurt, you need to be extra gentle with exercises. It's like when you scrape your knee—you clean it carefully at first, then treat it normally when it's better.

Detailed Explanation (Encyclopedia)

During the acute phase of rehabilitation, tissues are sensitive and require a cautious approach. ELDOA practitioners emphasize gentle decompression techniques, encouraging small movements and supported positions to alleviate stress without provoking pain. This phase prioritizes pain reduction and calming the nervous system.

Additionally, positions are modified with pillows or props to reduce load on irritated segments. Breathing is integrated deliberately, as diaphragmatic breathing can assist in balancing tension and activating the parasympathetic response. Holds are kept to approximately one minute, enough to begin viscoelastic change without overwhelming inflamed tissues.

Consequently, progression from the acute to functional phases depends on symptom improvement rather than a fixed timeline. Markers such as decreased pain at rest, increased tolerance to sustained positions, and the ability to maintain posture without compensations indicate readiness to advance. This patient‑centered approach helps decrease strain, enhance recovery, and build a foundation for more challenging exercises.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the acute phase protocol?

It is a series of guidelines for practicing ELDOA in the first weeks following an injury. The focus is on gentle decompression, proper breathing, and supported positions to help tissues heal.

Why is gentle decompression important after injury?

Injured tissues are easily irritated. Gentle decompression alleviates stress without provoking pain, allowing healing to begin and preventing further damage.

How does ELDOA modify positions for the acute phase?

Positions are supported with props to reduce load. The range is limited to comfortable zones, and emphasis is placed on relaxed breathing and awareness rather than maximal stretch.

How long does this phase last?

Usually two to four weeks. However, progression is based on symptom improvement—such as reduced pain at rest and better tolerance of holds—rather than a strict timeline.

Can I perform regular ELDOA positions during this phase?

Regular positions may need modification or may be postponed. Focus on gentle versions until symptoms subside, then gradually reintroduce more challenging exercises.

Are there specific breathing techniques?

Yes. Slow, deep diaphragmatic breathing promotes relaxation, assists in balancing tension, and supports healing. Maintain a steady rhythm throughout each hold.
