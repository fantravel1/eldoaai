Asymmetries

When one side of your body differs from the other, like being right‑handed. Minor differences are normal, but large imbalances need attention.

ELDOA Encyclopedia

Athletes and everyday movers alike develop asymmetries in strength and mobility. In baseball pitchers, a glenohumeral internal rotation deficit affects 70–85% of collegiate athletes, creating 15–20° differences between shoulders and altering throwing mechanics【844102180325802†L899-L914】. Research documents 14.4° less scapular posterior tilt during pitching and thoracolumbar rotation differences with an effect size of 0.61 favouring the non‑throwing side【844102180325802†L899-L914】. While such adaptations may aid performance, they also increase injury risk and cause long‑term dysfunction if left unchecked.

ELDOA’s approach to asymmetries recognizes that perfect symmetry is neither achievable nor necessary. Instead, practitioners seek to optimise function within the demands of a sport while preventing extreme differences. Targeted segmental protocols—such as T4–T8 decompressions for rotational restrictions or C5–C6 exercises for cervical extension from throwing—address specific limitations. Crucially, assessment identifies which asymmetries are beneficial adaptations versus dysfunctional compensations, guiding the intervention accordingly. Compensatory patterns above or below a primary adaptation often cause more problems than the initial imbalance, so whole‑body evaluation is essential【844102180325802†L899-L914】.

The progressive nature of ELDOA allows gradual normalization of harmful patterns without destabilizing finely tuned movement skills. Instead of aggressively forcing symmetry, practitioners systematically improve mobility where it’s lacking, enhance control where it’s excessive, and respect the necessary adaptations that enable high performance. This balanced strategy reduces injury risk while preserving the unique qualities that make each athlete effective.

T4–T8 Rotational Balance Drill

Hold a seated decompression for the mid‑thoracic spine, then rotate slowly to each side. Focus on equal rotation and identify restrictions or tension differences.

C5–C6 Unilateral Decompression

Perform a cervical decompression on one side at a time to restore balance between the neck segments affected by unilateral sports like pitching or serving.

Unilateral Lower Limb Decompression

Work one leg at a time in a hip decoaptation drill to correct differences in hip mobility and reduce compensatory tension in the lumbar spine.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are asymmetries?

They are differences between the left and right sides of your body. These may be in strength, flexibility or movement patterns and often develop from habits, sport or injury.

Are some asymmetries normal?

Yes. Not all differences are problematic. For instance, throwing athletes naturally develop dominant‑side adaptations. ELDOA aims to address only those asymmetries that create harmful compensations or injury risk【844102180325802†L899-L914】.

How can asymmetries lead to injury?

Large differences in rotation or mobility force other parts of the body to compensate, overloading joints and tissues. In pitchers, a 15–20° glenohumeral internal rotation deficit alters mechanics and increases stress on the elbow and shoulder【844102180325802†L899-L914】.

How does ELDOA address asymmetries?

By identifying which differences are beneficial versus harmful and prescribing segmental decompression exercises to restore mobility where it’s lacking. Exercises for T4–T8, C5–C6 and hips rebalance the body without sacrificing useful adaptations【844102180325802†L899-L914】.

Should I try to eliminate all asymmetry?

No. The goal is to optimize your body within its sport or lifestyle demands. Completely eliminating asymmetry may actually reduce performance. Focus on balancing harmful compensations and maintaining useful differences.

Which sports create the biggest asymmetries?

Sports with unilateral patterns, like baseball, tennis and golf, create significant side‑to‑side differences. Research notes scapular and thoracolumbar rotation differences in pitchers, which ELDOA addresses through targeted protocols【844102180325802†L899-L914】.
