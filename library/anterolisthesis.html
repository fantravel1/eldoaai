Anterolisthesis

Detailed Explanation

Anterolisthesis describes a vertebra sliding forward on the one below. The result is added shear across the disc and facet joints, with muscles and fascia bracing to protect the area. ELDOA’s segmental decompression reduces compressive load, improves joint orientation, and helps the nervous system adopt a calmer, more efficient strategy.

Programming typically begins at the involved level (often L5–S1 or L4–L5) and the thoracolumbar junction to improve global balance. One-minute sustained holds with quiet nasal breathing cultivate axial length and proprioception so day-to-day movements—standing, walking, lifting—require less bracing and feel steadier.

Frequently Asked Questions

What causes anterolisthesis?

Degenerative changes, congenital or stress-related pars issues, or traumatic events can allow a vertebra to slip forward.

Does ELDOA replace medical care?

No. ELDOA is an exercise method. Work with your healthcare professional for diagnosis and shared decision-making.

How should I breathe during holds?

Quiet, low nasal breathing encourages rib-pelvis organization and helps down-regulate protective tone.

Can I practice during flare-ups?

Use supported, pain-free ranges and shorter holds; stop if symptoms increase. Consult your clinician if unsure.

How long until I notice changes?

Many notice less gripping and better standing tolerance within 1–2 weeks of daily practice; durable changes accrue over months.

Which movements should I avoid early on?

Aggressive back extension or loaded hyperextension. Favor neutral alignment and controlled ranges.

Do I need equipment?

No—just space to set up precisely. Bolsters or blocks can help support positions comfortably.
