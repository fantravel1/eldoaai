The digital age has introduced a new epidemic of cervical spine dysfunction. Whether you call it "tech neck," "text neck," or "smartphone neck," the underlying problem is the same: chronic forward head posture caused by prolonged device use that fundamentally alters the biomechanics of the cervical spine.

"Text neck and/or tech neck is currently the most problematic work-related issue today."
— Ben Velazquez, ELDOA Expert

This comprehensive guide provides self-assessment tests to evaluate your cervical health, ELDOA protocols specifically designed for tech neck correction, and prevention strategies for long-term spinal wellness in our device-dependent world.

Understanding the Biomechanics

The human head weighs approximately 10-12 pounds (4.5-5.5 kg) when balanced directly over the spine. However, the physics of forward head posture dramatically increase the effective load on the cervical spine:

0-15 degrees 12 lbs effective weight - Normal range
15-30 degrees 27 lbs effective weight - Early compensation
30-45 degrees 40 lbs effective weight - Moderate stress
45-60 degrees 49-60 lbs effective weight - Severe strain

This increased mechanical load creates a cascade of adaptations:

  • Upper cervical hyperextension: C0-C2 segments extend to keep eyes level
  • Lower cervical flexion: C4-C7 segments flex forward, losing natural lordosis
  • Posterior muscle strain: Suboccipitals, upper trapezius, and levator scapulae become overworked
  • Anterior muscle shortening: SCM, scalenes, and deep neck flexors tighten
  • Disc compression: Anterior disc loading increases degeneration risk
  • Neural tension: Cervical nerve roots and brachial plexus may become irritated

The Tech Neck Self-Assessment Battery

The following tests help evaluate the degree of tech neck dysfunction and identify which specific structures are most affected. Perform all tests and note your results to track progress over time.

1 The Wall Test (Forward Head Posture Assessment)
This simple test reveals the degree of forward head posture and the adaptations that have occurred in the cervical spine.
How to Perform
  1. Stand with your back against a flat wall
  2. Place heels approximately 4 inches from the wall
  3. Press your buttocks and shoulder blades against the wall
  4. Do NOT force your head back—let it rest naturally
  5. Have someone measure the distance from the wall to the back of your head
  6. Note if touching your head to the wall requires effort or causes discomfort
Interpretation
  • 0-1 inch (0-2.5 cm): Normal - head naturally touches or nearly touches wall
  • 1-2 inches (2.5-5 cm): Mild forward head posture - early tech neck
  • 2-3 inches (5-7.5 cm): Moderate forward head posture - established tech neck
  • 3+ inches (7.5+ cm): Severe forward head posture - advanced tech neck
2 Chin Tuck Endurance Test
Evaluates the endurance capacity of the deep neck flexors (longus colli and longus capitis), which are often weakened in tech neck.
How to Perform
  1. Lie on your back with knees bent
  2. Perform a chin tuck (make a "double chin")
  3. Lift your head 1 inch off the floor while maintaining the chin tuck
  4. Hold this position as long as possible
  5. Stop when you cannot maintain the chin tuck or feel excessive strain
  6. Record the hold time in seconds
Interpretation
  • 60+ seconds: Good deep neck flexor endurance
  • 30-60 seconds: Moderate weakness - intervention recommended
  • 15-30 seconds: Significant weakness - tech neck likely contributing
  • Under 15 seconds: Severe weakness - priority rehabilitation needed
3 Cervical Rotation Range Test
Assesses rotational mobility of the cervical spine. Restrictions often develop at C1-C2 (primary rotation) and lower cervical segments in tech neck.
How to Perform
  1. Sit upright with good posture
  2. Keep shoulders facing forward and level
  3. Rotate your head to the right as far as possible without moving shoulders
  4. Note the end point (ideally, chin should nearly reach the shoulder line)
  5. Return to center and rotate left
  6. Compare both sides for symmetry
Interpretation
  • 70-90 degrees each way: Normal rotation range
  • 50-70 degrees: Mild restriction - early fascial adhesions
  • 30-50 degrees: Moderate restriction - significant dysfunction
  • Under 30 degrees: Severe restriction - medical evaluation recommended
  • Asymmetry over 10 degrees: Segmental dysfunction likely
4 Upper Trapezius Palpation Test
Evaluates tension and trigger points in the upper trapezius, one of the most affected muscles in tech neck syndrome.
How to Perform
  1. Locate the upper trapezius (the muscle at the top of your shoulder)
  2. Using the opposite hand, press firmly into the muscle belly
  3. Rate the tenderness on a scale of 0-10
  4. Note any "knots" or taut bands in the tissue
  5. Check if pressure reproduces any referred pain (headache, arm tingling)
  6. Repeat on the opposite side
Interpretation
  • 0-2 tenderness, no nodules: Normal muscle tone
  • 3-5 tenderness, mild nodules: Moderate tension - common with regular device use
  • 6-8 tenderness, prominent nodules: Significant trigger points - active tech neck
  • 9-10 tenderness with referred symptoms: Severe - comprehensive intervention needed
5 Thoracic Extension Test
Tech neck is almost always accompanied by thoracic hyperkyphosis. This test evaluates thoracic mobility which directly affects cervical mechanics.
How to Perform
  1. Sit in a chair with your buttocks against the back of the seat
  2. Clasp hands behind your head
  3. Lean back over the chair, extending your thoracic spine
  4. Keep your lower back stable (don't arch the lumbar spine)
  5. Note the range of motion and any restrictions or pain
  6. Observe if one segment feels particularly "stuck"
Interpretation
  • Smooth extension, approximately 25-30 degrees: Normal thoracic mobility
  • Limited extension with global stiffness: General hyperkyphosis
  • Focal restriction at specific level: Segmental hypomobility
  • Pain with extension: Possible facet joint involvement

Interpreting Your Results

Scoring Summary

Count how many tests showed moderate or worse dysfunction:

  • 0-1 tests positive: Minimal tech neck - focus on prevention
  • 2-3 tests positive: Developing tech neck - proactive intervention recommended
  • 4-5 tests positive: Established tech neck - comprehensive protocol needed

ELDOA Protocol for Tech Neck

The following ELDOA exercises specifically target the segments most affected by tech neck. They should be performed daily, ideally in the order presented.

1 ELDOA C5-C6 Lower Cervical
The C5-C6 segment is one of the most commonly affected in tech neck, often showing early degenerative changes due to the mechanical stress of forward head posture.
Execution Steps
  1. Sit with back against a wall, legs straight in front
  2. Flex feet strongly (dorsiflex ankles)
  3. Push knees toward floor (fully extend)
  4. Press lower back into wall while maintaining slight lumbar curve
  5. Perform chin tuck, creating length in posterior neck
  6. Push crown of head toward ceiling while chin stays tucked
  7. Slightly rotate head LEFT and side-bend LEFT
  8. Push through hands at sides to depress shoulders
  9. Hold with maximum tension while breathing
Duration: 60 seconds
Sets: 1-2
Frequency: Daily
2 ELDOA C6-C7 Cervicothoracic Junction
The C6-C7 segment forms the transition to the thoracic spine and bears significant load in forward head posture. This ELDOA targets the critical cervicothoracic junction.
Execution Steps
  1. Sit with back against wall, legs extended
  2. Strongly dorsiflex feet and extend knees
  3. Press lower back into wall
  4. Perform strong chin tuck
  5. Push crown toward ceiling
  6. Slightly rotate head RIGHT and side-bend RIGHT
  7. Depress shoulders maximally, spreading fingers wide
  8. Create opposition between crown and sitting bones
  9. Maintain position while breathing into lower ribs
Duration: 60 seconds
Sets: 1-2
Frequency: Daily
3 ELDOA T1-T2 Upper Thoracic
Tech neck always involves the upper thoracic spine. This ELDOA creates space in the upper thoracic segments that become compressed with chronic forward posture.
Execution Steps
  1. Sit against wall with legs extended
  2. Flex feet and extend knees fully
  3. Press entire spine against wall
  4. Perform chin tuck and push crown up
  5. Reach arms forward at shoulder height
  6. Round the upper back while pushing hands forward
  7. Keep crown pushing up despite the forward reach
  8. Spread shoulder blades apart (protract)
  9. Maintain tension while breathing
Duration: 60 seconds
Sets: 1-2
Frequency: Daily
4 ELDOA T4-T5 Mid Thoracic
The T4-T5 region is the apex of the thoracic kyphosis and becomes exaggerated in tech neck. This ELDOA helps restore proper thoracic curvature.
Execution Steps
  1. Sit on floor, legs crossed or extended
  2. Reach arms overhead, interlacing fingers
  3. Turn palms toward ceiling
  4. Push hands toward ceiling while sitting bones push down
  5. Slightly flex the upper back to target T4-T5
  6. Create maximum axial extension
  7. Pull elbows slightly back behind ears
  8. Maintain chin tuck throughout
  9. Hold with consistent tension while breathing
Duration: 60 seconds
Sets: 1-2
Frequency: Daily

Complementary Exercises

Deep Neck Flexor Strengthening

Weak deep neck flexors contribute to forward head posture. This exercise strengthens the muscles that support proper head position.

  1. Lie on your back with knees bent
  2. Perform a chin tuck (create a double chin)
  3. Nod your head slightly as if saying "yes" very subtly
  4. Hold the tucked position for 10 seconds
  5. Release and repeat 10-15 times
  6. Progress to head lift (1 inch off floor) once proficient

Upper Trapezius Stretch

Relieves the chronic tension that develops in the upper trapezius from compensating for forward head posture.

  1. Sit or stand with good posture
  2. Reach your right hand behind your back
  3. Tilt your head to the left (ear toward shoulder)
  4. Gently add overpressure with your left hand on your head
  5. Rotate your nose slightly upward to intensify
  6. Hold for 30-60 seconds; repeat 2-3 times each side

Thoracic Extension over Foam Roller

Restores thoracic extension mobility that is lost with chronic kyphosis.

  1. Place foam roller horizontally on the floor
  2. Lie with the roller at your upper thoracic spine (between shoulder blades)
  3. Support your head with your hands
  4. Gently extend over the roller, letting gravity assist
  5. Move the roller up or down to target different segments
  6. Perform 2-3 extensions at each level

Prevention Strategies

Workstation Setup Checklist

  • Monitor at eye level, approximately arm's length away
  • Keyboard and mouse at elbow height
  • Feet flat on floor or footrest
  • Chair supporting natural spinal curves
  • Screen glare minimized to reduce forward leaning
  • External keyboard and monitor for laptop users

The 20-20-20 Rule

Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This breaks the sustained forward gaze pattern and allows postural reset.

Phone Positioning Protocol

  • Raise phone to eye level when possible
  • Use voice commands to reduce looking down
  • Limit continuous phone use to 10-15 minute intervals
  • Use tablet stands or phone holders at eye level
  • Practice the chin tuck while using devices

Movement Breaks

Every 30-60 minutes, perform a brief movement break:

  1. Chin tucks: 10 repetitions
  2. Shoulder rolls: 10 forward, 10 backward
  3. Thoracic rotation: 5 each side
  4. Chest opener: Arms behind back, squeeze shoulder blades, 30 seconds
  5. One ELDOA of your choice

When to Seek Professional Help

Consult a healthcare provider if you experience:

  • Numbness, tingling, or weakness in arms or hands
  • Headaches that worsen with position or activity
  • Significant pain that doesn't improve with rest
  • Dizziness or visual disturbances with neck movement
  • Progressive limitation in range of motion

Tracking Progress

Re-assess using the test battery every 4-6 weeks to track improvement. Keep a log of your results:

  • Wall test distance (inches)
  • Chin tuck endurance (seconds)
  • Cervical rotation (degrees each side)
  • Upper trapezius tenderness (0-10)
  • Thoracic extension quality

With consistent ELDOA practice and lifestyle modifications, most people see measurable improvement within 4-8 weeks.

Conclusion

Tech neck is not inevitable, even in our device-dependent world. Through regular self-assessment, targeted ELDOA practice, and mindful device use habits, you can prevent and reverse the cervical dysfunction that plagues modern life.

The key is consistency: daily ELDOA practice of just 10-15 minutes, combined with ergonomic awareness and movement breaks, provides the foundation for lasting cervical health. Your spine evolved for movement and variety—give it what it needs, and it will reward you with improved posture, reduced pain, and better function for decades to come.
